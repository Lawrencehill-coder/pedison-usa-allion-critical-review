3. Disclosure Element Deep-Dive, Entity by Entity

DLP Capital — DLP Lending Fund (506(c)-only, closest match)

  • Not-an-offer language in full caps at the page bottom
  • States any offer is made only by means of a PPM to accredited investors
  • States the website excludes material information contained in the PPM, including risk factors
  • States plainly there is no guarantee of return of capital
  • Forward-looking statements caveat tied to specific footnotes on every target-return figure shown
  • Discloses the investment is not a bank deposit and is not FDIC-insured
  • States neither the SEC nor any state regulator has approved or recommended the investment
  • Every performance figure (IRR, preferred return, target return) is footnoted to a numbered disclosure
  • Full disclosure block appears at the bottom of every fund page, not just once site-wide

PassiveInvesting.com — Real Estate Debt Fund / PIC Fund I (gated landing-page model)

  • Confirms Rule 506(c), accredited investors only, with specific net worth and income thresholds spelled out in plain language
  • States the offering is also available under Regulation A for certain terms (same sponsor, parallel exemptions, each with its own disclosure requirements)
  • Gated content model: the actual offering memorandum is not shown until a prospect submits a lead form
  • States explicitly that non-accredited investors will not be allowed to participate
  • States any offer occurs only through a PPM, a determination of suitability, independent verification, and a subscription agreement (a four-part gate)
  • Closing acknowledgment line: by accessing the website, the visitor agrees to the terms of the disclaimer
  • Standard risk disclosures: loss of principal, illiquidity, no guarantee of returns, past performance not indicative of future results
  • Built on a lead-generation funnel platform rather than a standalone custom HTML build, relevant to the recommendation in Section 6

EquityMultiple (marketplace + affiliated broker-dealer)

  • Gates access to accredited investors under Rule 501 of Regulation D
  • Discloses which exemption applies per offering (varies by deal, not one blanket statement)
  • Discloses its Alpine Notes product is not FDIC-insured and does not carry a guaranteed return
  • Fee disclosure is explicit and specific per product, not one blanket number
  • Independent third-party reviews are currently mixed, a reminder that aggregate review scores only help if the underlying reputation supports them

RealtyMogul — Income REIT + private placements (SEC-registered adviser)

  • REIT product operates under a qualified Form 1-A offering circular, filed with and reviewed by the SEC (a materially higher bar than a 506(c) notice filing)
  • Discloses a specific 10% of income or net worth investment cap for non-accredited investors, citing the exact Regulation A rule
  • Discloses the REIT is managed by an SEC-registered investment adviser
  • Discloses an affiliated broker-dealer that performs diligence on each sponsor and deal before listing
  • Individual private placements are separately labeled as Rule 506(c) offerings requiring investor-level accreditation verification

Phoenix Energy (previously benchmarked, heaviest-weight comparable)

  • Full disclosure block repeated on every page, sized for 506(c) + Form S-1 + a financing subsidiary offering all at once
  • Discloses its broker-dealer relationship (Crescent Securities Group, FINRA/SIPC)
  • SEC oil & gas reserve reporting rules and non-GAAP financial measure disclosures (industry-specific, not relevant to Allion, but a useful example of scope matching complexity)
  • Mainly useful as an upper bound

5. Critical Evaluation, by Regulatory Layer

Federal (SEC) layer

  • No visible not-an-offer, accredited-investor-only, or PPM-only language anywhere on the live Allion Fund pages, though every comparable carries this without exception
  • Rule 506(c) allows general solicitation, but that comes paired with an obligation to take reasonable steps to verify accreditation and avoid materially misleading statements in whatever is publicly solicited
  • No risk factors summary on the public page at all
  • No forward-looking statements caveat tied to the preferred-return figures, even though the 8–10% range strongly suggests these are targets, not fixed terms

State layer

  • Rule 506(c) offerings are exempt from state registration under federal preemption, but not from state notice filing requirements
  • Most states still require a Form D copy notice filing through NASAA's Electronic Filing Depository, plus a filing fee in many states (Florida is the one exception)
  • Since our Form D was only filed April 9, 2026 with $0 sold, worth confirming state notice filings are actually completed in Texas and everywhere else we have solicited or expect to solicit, especially given general solicitation is in use
  • We should confirm the Texas State Securities Board notice filing has been made separately from the federal Form D
  • None of our live Allion pages reference Texas or federal regulatory status at all, even though this language already exists in the teaser built separately in this conversation

Consistency and recordkeeping layer

  • Suite 105 versus Suite 110 address inconsistency should be resolved, with one address used everywhere: the Form D, the website, business cards, and every investor-facing document
  • No centralized disclosure block currently used consistently across all Allion materials. The teaser built in this conversation carries a complete footer; the live pedisonusa.com Allion pages do not

6. HTML Landing Pages: Individualized-Per-Investor vs. a Standardized Path

The good news

Individualized outreach is not, by itself, a 506(c) problem. Rule 506(c) exists specifically to permit general solicitation and advertising, including targeted outreach to named individual prospects and personalized landing pages. What creates risk is what's on the page (or missing from it), and how consistently that content is controlled across every version that goes out.

Where the real risk lives: version sprawl, not personalization

  • Each new one-off page is a separate piece of solicitation material that must independently carry the full, current disclosure set
  • If disclosure language ever gets corrected after counsel review, every previously built one-off page needs the same update, and older pages can keep circulating with stale disclosures if there's no central inventory of what's live
  • With no version control, it's hard to answer a simple compliance question after the fact: what did a specific investor actually see, and when
  • Personalization of a name, a specific deal like Red Oak, or a return target is fine. What should never vary between versions is the disclosure block, the risk factors, or the accredited-investor gating language

What the comparables actually do (none build bespoke pages per prospect)

  • DLP and Phoenix run a small, fixed set of fund pages, one per active fund, each carrying the full disclosure block, and personalize the outreach rather than the page
  • PassiveInvesting.com runs a single gated landing page per offering on a repeatable funnel template, with the disclosure block baked into the template
  • RealtyMogul separates by product type, not by individual investor, with each product type carrying its own centrally controlled disclosure language

Recommendation

  1. Maintain one canonical, disclosure-complete master page per active offering (currently just Allion Fund 1), with the disclosure footer treated as a non-negotiable, unchanging block
  2. Personalize the outreach, not the disclosure. Add investor- or deal-specific callouts as a section within the canonical page or a linked one-pager, rather than duplicating the whole page
  3. Keep a simple distribution log: which version of which page was sent to which prospect, and when
  4. Route any new one-off page through the same disclosure template used on the canonical page
  5. Have counsel bless one master disclosure block (not-an-offer language, accredited-investor gate, risk factors, forward-looking statements, state notices) and treat it as locked, pasted in unedited everywhere

This gets us a standardized, defensible, single point of update, without giving up the ability to send individualized, targeted outreach, which is exactly what 506(c) is designed to allow.

Key definitions

TermWhat it means here
Canonical master pageThe one authoritative, version-controlled page for a given offering. Every other piece of outreach links back to it or copies its disclosure block verbatim, rather than becoming its own independent page.
Disclosure block / footerThe fixed set of legal language (not-an-offer language, accredited-investor gate, risk factors, forward-looking statements, state notices) that must appear identically wherever the offering is described. This block is locked and only changes with counsel approval.
Version sprawlThe risk created when many independent copies of a page exist with no central control, so disclosure language can drift, go stale, or go missing across different versions over time.
Distribution logA simple record (a spreadsheet is enough) noting which version of which page was sent to which prospect, and when, so we can reconstruct what any investor actually saw if it's ever needed.
Noindex / canonical tagHTML instructions placed in a page's code that tell search engines not to index a supplemental page, and to treat another URL (the master page) as the authoritative version instead.

7. Website Architecture vs. Adding Landing Pages: Ranking and Search Considerations

This is a separate question from the disclosure question above. Even if every one-off page carried perfect disclosures, how we structure those pages on the web affects how search engines treat pedisonusa.com as a whole, and it affects how professional the operation looks to anyone who stumbles across more than one version of it.

Why this matters technically

  • Domain authority is not per-page, it's cumulative. Every backlink, every bit of trust a search engine assigns, accrues to a domain over time. A page built as its own standalone Netlify site (a new subdomain like pedison-allion-investorX.netlify.app) starts with zero authority of its own and does nothing to strengthen pedisonusa.com's standing. A page built as a folder under pedisonusa.com (like pedisonusa.com/allionfund/) inherits the authority of the parent domain instead.
  • Duplicate and near-duplicate content gets suppressed, not doubled up. If the same offering description, the same return figures, and the same disclosure block appear across many separate one-off pages, search engines generally treat this as duplicate content and will index only one version, often not the one we'd want surfaced, while quietly discounting the rest.
  • Doorway pages are a named policy problem. Google's search guidelines specifically flag "doorway pages," meaning many slightly different pages built to funnel visitors toward the same destination, as a practice that can suppress or penalize a domain's visibility. A pattern of one bespoke HTML page per investor or per campaign, all saying nearly the same thing, resembles this pattern structurally even when the intent is personalization rather than manipulation. This is a reputational and technical risk on top of the disclosure risk already covered in Section 6.
  • Index bloat dilutes relevance signals. Every additional indexed page competes with our own other pages for the same keywords (Allion Fund, real estate debt fund, Dallas, accredited investors, and so on). More near-identical pages generally means weaker rankings for all of them, not stronger.

What this means in practice for Allion

  • The canonical master page described in Section 6 should live as a proper part of pedisonusa.com's site architecture (for example, a folder under the existing domain) rather than as an independent one-off subdomain, so it builds the domain's authority instead of starting from zero every time.
  • Personalized outreach (an email, a deal-specific one-pager) should link to that one canonical page, using tracked parameters if we want to know who came from where, rather than becoming its own separately indexed page.
  • If a deal-specific or investor-specific page is genuinely needed as a standalone artifact (for example, to attach to an email or share outside a browser), it can carry a noindex tag and a canonical link tag pointing back to the master page, so search engines don't treat it as a competing or duplicate destination even though a human can still view it directly.
  • None of this changes the recommendation in Section 6. It reinforces it: the same one-master-page structure that solves the disclosure consistency problem also solves the search and reputation problem.

8. Recommended Procedure for Individual Investor Outreach

A concrete, repeatable process for building the master page once, and for sending targeted outreach to a named prospect afterward, without creating a new disclosure risk each time.

Step by step: building the master page

  1. Draft the content. Fund overview, terms, and any project spotlight, using Pedison's standard brand template.
  2. Insert the disclosure block verbatim. Paste in the counsel-approved disclosure language exactly as approved. Never edit it ad hoc while building the page.
  3. Publish it inside pedisonusa.com's own site architecture. A folder or path under the existing domain (for example, pedisonusa.com/allionfund/), not a standalone subdomain, so it inherits the domain's authority rather than starting from zero (see Section 7).
  4. Version and date-stamp it. Add a version number and last-updated date in the footer, and keep the source file in a version-controlled location so past versions are recoverable.

Step by step: building an individual outreach page for one investor

  1. Start from the canonical master page. Confirm the current, counsel-approved version and its URL before beginning any outreach.
  2. Copy the disclosure block verbatim. Do not retype, summarize, or edit it. If a supplemental page needs the disclosure text physically present, paste it exactly as it appears on the master page's current version.
  3. Add personalization only. The investor's name, relevant context (for example, interest in Red Oak), and a greeting. Nothing about the terms, risk factors, or disclosure language should differ from the master page.
  4. Tag it noindex with a canonical link back to the master page. This keeps the supplemental page from competing with or diluting the master page in search results (see Section 7).
  5. Use a tracked link if attribution is needed, instead of building a separate page just to track who clicked.
  6. Log the send. Investor name, page/version, link, and date, in the shared distribution log.
  7. If the conversation surfaces a need for new or different disclosure language, stop and route it to Legal first (see Section 9). Never add new disclosure language directly to a one-off page.

9. Legal Counsel Review and Update Management

This section covers who reviews disclosure changes, when, and how an approved update actually gets managed across every page it touches, so this doesn't become a one-time exercise that quietly goes stale.

What gets routed to counsel

  • Any new disclosure language, or any change to existing disclosure language, before it goes live anywhere
  • Any new offering, product, or fund added alongside Allion Fund 1, before its page is published
  • Any state where we begin soliciting investors for the first time, to confirm notice filing requirements are met
  • Any individual investor conversation that raises a question the current disclosure language does not clearly answer

What does not need a fresh review each time

  • Personalizing outreach with an investor's name, a specific deal reference, or a greeting, none of which touches disclosure language
  • Reusing the already-approved disclosure block verbatim on a new supplemental page
  • Routine factual updates that do not touch terms, risk, or regulatory language (for example, a project photo or a contact phone number)

How an approved update is managed once counsel signs off

  1. Counsel approves the exact wording and the version is dated and recorded.
  2. The master page is updated first, and only the master page. No other page should ever get updated wording before the master page does.
  3. Every existing supplemental or one-off page that duplicates the disclosure text in full is identified from the distribution log and updated to match, or retired in favor of a link back to the master page.
  4. The distribution log is annotated to show which version was current as of the update date, so anything sent before that date can be identified if ever needed.
  5. The team is notified that a new approved version exists, so nobody continues sending outreach built on outdated language.

The practical effect: counsel touches the wording exactly once per change, and that one approval propagates everywhere through the master page and the log, rather than requiring a separate review for every individual investor page in circulation.
